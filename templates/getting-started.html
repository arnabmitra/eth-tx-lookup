What is Gamma Exposure (GEX)?

Gamma Exposure (GEX), also known as Gamma Levels, is a specialized metric that measures the change in delta exposure for options based on changes in the underlying price. It provides insight into potential market maker activities, helping traders anticipate market trends.

Function and Market Maker Hedging

GEX highlights important price levels where significant gamma exists due to market positioning and open interest.

  • GEX reflects where market-makers and dealers may need to hedge to mitigate their risk.
  • In simpler terms, it shows how much market makers and dealers will need to buy or sell of an underlying asset to hedge their positions as the price moves.
  • This hedging, known as Delta Hedging, is a strategy market makers use to manage risks from price changes in underlying assets, maintaining balanced positions to ensure market stability and liquidity.

Interpreting GEX: The "Price Magnet" Analogy

GEX can be conceptualized as a gravitational pull on the stock price. The net GEX position dictates whether market maker activity will stabilize or accelerate price movements:

GEX Condition Market Effect Dealer Action & Price Impact Volatility
Positive GEX (Long Gamma) Acts like a stabilizer or price magnet. Highlighted in green. Dealers will buy as prices fall and sell as prices rise to hedge their positions. This creates support on the downside. Low overall volatility.
Negative GEX (Short Gamma) Acts like an accelerator. Highlighted in red. Dealers will sell as prices fall and buy as prices rise. This amplifies price swings and can lead to accelerated moves to the downside. Higher volatility.

GEX theory suggests that the underlying asset tends to gravitate towards the strikes with the highest gamma exposure, which can act as a supply/resistance zone (calls) or a demand/support zone (puts).

Key GEX Levels and Quantification

  • Zero Gamma Level (Gamma Flip Point): This is the tipping point where a change in the market's hedging dynamics can be signaled, shifting the environment from stable to volatile, or vice-versa.
  • Data Calculation: GEX is calculated and updated throughout the trading day. By default, calculations are often based on a 1% move of the underlying security using gamma and open interest.
  • Quantification Example: A GEX of $500M signifies that for every 1% move in the underlying stock, market makers would theoretically need to buy or sell $500 million worth of the stock to remain hedged.
  • Formula Components: GEX is calculated by adding up all Call and Put Gamma at each strike level to present the net Gamma Exposure. The calculation of the total option's change in delta based on a 1% move can involve: Option's Gamma * Open Interest * Spot Price * Spot Price.

Step-by-Step: Your First GEX Analysis

1

Choose Your Ticker

Start with liquid, actively traded tickers where options volume is high. The best tickers for GEX analysis are:

  • SPY: Most liquid ETF, perfect for learning GEX
  • QQQ: Tech-heavy index, shows clear GEX patterns
  • AAPL, TSLA, NVDA: Individual stocks with massive options activity
  • IWM: Small-cap index, different dynamics than large caps

üí° Pro Tip:

Start with SPY. It has the most options volume, making GEX levels very reliable and clear to interpret.

2

Select the Expiration Date

The expiration date determines which options contracts you're analyzing. Different expirations tell different stories:

  • This Week's Friday (0DTE-3DTE): Shows immediate dealer positioning, best for day trading
  • Next Week (1-2 weeks out): Good balance of volume and forward-looking positioning
  • Monthly/Quarterly OPEX: Shows major institutional positioning, key for swing trades
  • All Expiries Combined: Full picture of dealer exposure across time (use our All Expiries tool)

üí° Pro Tip:

For day trading, focus on the nearest Friday expiration. For swing trading, look at monthly OPEX (3rd Friday of the month).

3

Read the GEX Chart

Once your chart loads, here's what you're looking at:

Green Bars (Positive GEX)

These represent strikes where dealers have POSITIVE gamma exposure. This means:

  • Dealers sell when price goes up, buy when price goes down
  • Creates a stabilizing "pin" effect
  • Acts as support/resistance
  • The taller the bar, the stronger the effect

Red Bars (Negative GEX)

These represent strikes where dealers have NEGATIVE gamma exposure. This means:

  • Dealers buy when price goes up, sell when price goes down
  • Amplifies price movements (momentum fuel)
  • Can lead to "gamma squeezes"
  • Often occurs below the current price (put-heavy positioning)

üìä Chart Reading Example:

SPY is trading at $450. Your GEX chart shows:

  • Massive green bar at $450 strike (+$500M GEX)
  • Large green bar at $455 strike (+$300M GEX)
  • Moderate red bar at $445 strike (-$150M GEX)

Interpretation: $450 is strong support (dealers will defend it). $455 is resistance. If SPY breaks below $445, expect volatility to increase as dealers amplify the move.

4

Identify Key Levels

Look for these critical levels on your GEX chart:

The Largest Positive GEX Strike

This is often called the "pin level" or "gamma strike." Price has a tendency to gravitate toward this level, especially as expiration approaches. This is your primary reference point.

Zero Gamma Level

The price where GEX changes from positive to negative. Above this level = low volatility expected. Below this level = high volatility expected. This is a critical inflection point.

Put Wall vs. Call Wall

Large positive GEX concentrations above current price (call wall) act as resistance. Large positive GEX below current price (put wall) acts as support.

5

Apply to Your Trading

Now that you understand the levels, here's how to use them:

For Day Traders

  • Fade moves away from the largest positive GEX strike (expect mean reversion)
  • Be cautious trading through zero gamma‚Äîvolatility often spikes
  • Watch for breakouts above call walls or below put walls (often fail and reverse)
  • Use GEX levels as targets for profit-taking

For Options Traders

  • Sell options near high positive GEX strikes (high probability of pin)
  • Buy options when price is near zero gamma and likely to break out
  • Use GEX to determine expected move and price ranges
  • Avoid buying premium when trapped between strong GEX levels (choppy, range-bound)

For Swing Traders

  • Use monthly OPEX GEX levels to identify multi-week support/resistance
  • Look for accumulation near strong positive GEX support levels
  • Be cautious holding through OPEX when price is at major GEX pin
  • Combine GEX with traditional technical analysis for confluence

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Trading GEX Levels in Isolation

GEX is a powerful tool, but it's not magic. Always combine GEX analysis with price action, volume, news, and other technical indicators. GEX tells you WHERE dealers are positioned, not WHEN price will move there.

2. Ignoring Options Volume

GEX is most reliable on highly liquid tickers with massive options volume. On low-volume stocks, GEX levels can be misleading or change rapidly. Stick to SPY, QQQ, and mega-cap stocks when learning.

3. Assuming GEX Never Changes

GEX levels are dynamic and change throughout the day as new options are traded and positions are closed. Check GEX levels multiple times per day, especially before major moves or into the close.

4. Fighting the Trend with GEX

GEX creates support/resistance, but strong fundamental news or massive directional flow can overwhelm dealer hedging. If SPY is breaking above a call wall on huge volume and bullish news, don't blindly short expecting resistance.

5. Over-Trading Based on Small GEX Changes

Focus on the LARGEST GEX concentrations. Small bars on the chart don't have enough dealer positioning to meaningfully impact price action. Look for the obvious, glaring levels.

üöÄ Advanced Tips

Understanding OPEX Dynamics

On expiration day (especially monthly OPEX), GEX pinning effects are strongest. Price often gravitates toward the largest GEX strike as expiration approaches. This is called "gamma pinning" or "max pain."

üí° Pro Strategy:

On OPEX Friday, if price is within 1% of the largest positive GEX strike by 2pm ET, expect it to pin near that level into the close. Sell options (theta gang) or fade directional moves away from the pin.

Combining Multiple Expirations

Use our All Expiries GEX tool to see the combined gamma exposure across all dates. This shows the "total dealer positioning" and often reveals the strongest, most reliable support/resistance levels.

Volatility Regime Shifts

When overall market GEX flips from positive to negative, expect a regime change from low to high volatility. Monitor total market GEX (aggregate across SPY, QQQ, IWM) to gauge macro volatility expectations.

0DTE Options Impact

Same-day expiration (0DTE) options have exploded in volume recently. These create massive GEX concentrations that change rapidly throughout the day. For day traders, tracking 0DTE GEX on SPY/QQQ can explain sudden intraday support/resistance.
